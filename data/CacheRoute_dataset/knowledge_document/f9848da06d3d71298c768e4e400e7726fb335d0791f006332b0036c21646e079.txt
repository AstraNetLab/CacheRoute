A polygon in geometry is a two dimensional shape composed of straight
line segments connected end to end to form a closed figure, and the list
of polygons refers to the many named categories of polygons classified
according to the number of sides they contain. The simplest polygon is
the triangle which has three sides and three interior angles, and
triangles can be further categorized into equilateral, isosceles, and
scalene triangles depending on the relative lengths of their sides. A
polygon with four sides is called a quadrilateral and this group
includes shapes such as squares, rectangles, rhombuses, parallelograms,
trapezoids, and kites, each of which has specific geometric properties
relating to side lengths, angles, and parallel lines. A five sided
polygon is known as a pentagon, while a six sided polygon is called a
hexagon. A seven sided polygon is referred to as a heptagon or sometimes
a septagon in different mathematical traditions. An eight sided polygon
is an octagon, a nine sided polygon is a nonagon or enneagon, and a ten
sided polygon is known as a decagon. As the number of sides increases
the naming convention continues using prefixes derived largely from
Greek numerical roots, producing names such as hendecagon for an eleven
sided polygon and dodecagon for a twelve sided polygon. Beyond twelve
sides the list continues with triskaidecagon for thirteen sides,
tetradecagon for fourteen sides, pentadecagon for fifteen sides,
hexadecagon for sixteen sides, heptadecagon for seventeen sides,
octadecagon for eighteen sides, enneadecagon for nineteen sides, and
icosagon for a twenty sided polygon. In mathematics polygons with even
larger numbers of sides are also defined and studied, including shapes
such as a triacontagon with thirty sides, a tetracontagon with forty
sides, and a hecatonagon which has one hundred sides. These larger
polygons often appear in theoretical geometry, computer graphics,
architectural design, and mathematical visualization where many sided
figures can approximate circles. Polygons can also be classified
according to additional properties such as whether they are regular or
irregular. A regular polygon is one in which all sides have equal length
and all interior angles are equal, while an irregular polygon lacks this
uniformity. Regular polygons include familiar shapes such as the
equilateral triangle, square, regular pentagon, regular hexagon, and
regular octagon. Another important distinction is between convex
polygons and concave polygons. A convex polygon is one in which all
interior angles are less than one hundred eighty degrees and every line
segment connecting two points in the polygon lies entirely inside the
figure. A concave polygon contains at least one interior angle greater
than one hundred eighty degrees and may have parts that appear to fold
inward. Polygons also play a central role in many areas of mathematics
including geometry, topology, computational modeling, and computer
graphics. In digital graphics and video game design complex three
dimensional objects are often constructed from polygon meshes where
surfaces are approximated by many small polygons, typically triangles or
quadrilaterals. Architects and engineers also use polygonal forms in
structural design, tiling patterns, and geometric planning. Regular
polygons are frequently used in tessellations, where shapes repeat
without gaps across a plane. Examples include hexagonal tiling patterns
often found in honeycombs and square grids commonly used in architecture
and urban planning. Because polygons are fundamental geometric
structures they are introduced early in mathematics education and
continue to appear in advanced mathematical theory, including
discussions of symmetry, angle sums, coordinate geometry, and polygonal
