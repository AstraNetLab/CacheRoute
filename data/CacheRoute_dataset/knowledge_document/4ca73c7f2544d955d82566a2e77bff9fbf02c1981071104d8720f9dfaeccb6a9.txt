Iran is one of the most seismically active countries in the Middle East because it lies at the collision boundary between the Arabian and Eurasian tectonic plates, a geological setting that generates frequent and sometimes devastating earthquakes. Over centuries of recorded history, numerous earthquakes have struck different regions of the country, affecting cities, villages, and infrastructure and leaving lasting impacts on society, architecture, and disaster preparedness. Lists of earthquakes in Iran often include events dating from ancient historical records to modern instrumentally recorded earthquakes. These records illustrate both the frequency and severity of seismic activity across the Iranian plateau and surrounding regions. Historical chronicles indicate that large earthquakes occurred in areas such as Semnan, Tehran, and Ardabil as early as the first millennium. For example, an earthquake recorded in 856 near Semnan is estimated to have had a magnitude of about 7.9 and reportedly caused enormous destruction and loss of life, making it one of the earliest documented catastrophic earthquakes in the region. Other historical earthquakes include events near Tehran in the ninth century and near Bushehr in the tenth and eleventh centuries, which demonstrate that seismic hazards have long been present across multiple provinces. These early accounts were typically documented in historical chronicles, traveler accounts, and regional histories rather than by scientific instruments, yet they remain important sources for understanding the long-term seismicity of Iran.

Moving forward into the modern period, earthquakes continued to shape the historical development of the country. In the twentieth century, several destructive earthquakes were recorded using modern seismological instruments. One of the most notable early twentieth-century earthquakes occurred in 1909 near Delijan in central Iran and reached a magnitude of about 7.3. Another major earthquake occurred in 1930 in Salmas in northwestern Iran, causing significant destruction in towns and villages. In 1962 the Buin Zahra earthquake struck Qazvin Province with a magnitude of about 7.0 and caused widespread devastation, destroying many rural settlements and highlighting the vulnerability of traditional building structures to strong ground shaking. Earthquake researchers have frequently cited this event when discussing seismic risk in the region because the affected area lies along active fault systems that extend across northern Iran.

During the 1970s Iran experienced several powerful earthquakes that emphasized the country’s seismic risk. One of the most destructive was the 1978 Tabas earthquake in eastern Iran, which reached a magnitude of approximately 7.8 and resulted in tens of thousands of fatalities. This earthquake destroyed much of the city of Tabas and surrounding settlements and remains one of the strongest earthquakes ever recorded within Iran. The scale of the disaster drew international attention and demonstrated the catastrophic potential of earthquakes in densely populated regions with vulnerable infrastructure.

Another significant earthquake occurred in 1972 in southern Iran near the town of Qir. This earthquake measured around magnitude 7.1 and caused severe damage to communities in Fars Province. Numerous buildings collapsed due to the intensity of the shaking and the limitations of construction techniques used at the time. Such events reinforced the importance of earthquake engineering and the need for improved building standards in seismic regions. Over the following decades, Iran continued to experience earthquakes that varied in magnitude and impact, including events in Kerman, Golbaf, and other provinces. These earthquakes contributed to the development of Iran’s seismic monitoring networks and emergency response systems.

Perhaps the most devastating earthquake in modern Iranian history occurred on June 20, 1990, when a powerful earthquake struck the northern regions near the Caspian Sea, particularly the areas around Rudbar, Manjil, and Rasht. The earthquake had a magnitude estimated at approximately 7.3 to 7.4 and caused widespread destruction across Gilan and Zanjan provinces. Entire towns and villages were destroyed, and infrastructure such as roads, bridges, and power lines was severely damaged. Estimates suggest that between 40,000 and 50,000 people were killed and hundreds of thousands were left homeless. The disaster highlighted the urgent need for improved disaster preparedness and building safety standards in earthquake-prone regions.

In the late twentieth century and early twenty-first century, Iran continued to experience numerous earthquakes. The 1997 Qayen earthquake in eastern Iran reached a magnitude of about 7.3 and caused more than one thousand deaths and extensive damage to rural communities. This earthquake again illustrated the vulnerability of adobe and masonry buildings to strong seismic shaking. In 2003 another catastrophic earthquake struck the historic city of Bam in southeastern Iran. This earthquake measured approximately magnitude 6.6 but caused enormous devastation because it occurred close to a densely populated urban area with many traditional structures. The Bam earthquake destroyed much of the ancient citadel of Arg-e Bam and resulted in more than 20,000 deaths, making it one of the deadliest earthquakes in Iran in recent decades. The international community provided assistance in rescue operations and reconstruction efforts following the disaster.

Subsequent years also witnessed significant earthquakes across the country. In 2005 a magnitude 6.4 earthquake struck the Zarand region of Kerman Province, causing hundreds of deaths and widespread structural damage. In 2012 a pair of earthquakes in East Azerbaijan Province with magnitudes around 6.3 and 6.4 struck near the cities of Ahar and Varzaghan, killing hundreds of people and injuring thousands. These twin earthquakes, sometimes referred to as a seismic doublet, caused severe damage to villages in mountainous areas and posed challenges for rescue teams due to difficult terrain and transportation conditions.

More recently, earthquakes have continued to occur in Iran, though not all have caused catastrophic damage. In 2017 a powerful magnitude 7.3 earthquake struck near the Iran–Iraq border, killing hundreds of people and injuring thousands more. The earthquake generated numerous aftershocks and was felt across several neighboring countries, including Iraq, Turkey, and Syria. Although the epicenter was near the border region, the majority of casualties occurred in Iranian towns where buildings collapsed under the intense shaking. This event demonstrated once again the ongoing seismic hazard facing communities in western Iran.

Seismologists note that Iran experiences frequent earthquakes because of its location within a complex tectonic environment where several fault systems accommodate the convergence of major continental plates. The Arabian Plate moves northward toward the Eurasian Plate, compressing the crust and generating numerous faults across the Iranian plateau. As stress accumulates along these faults, it is periodically released through earthquakes. Scientific studies show that Iran has experienced dozens of earthquakes with magnitudes greater than 7.0 during the last century alone, along with many more moderate earthquakes between magnitudes 6.0 and 7.0.

In addition to large earthquakes, thousands of smaller tremors occur each year. Seismic monitoring networks record many of these events, some of which are too small to be felt by people but still contribute to scientific understanding of regional tectonics. Iranian seismological organizations and international research institutions continue to study fault systems, ground motion patterns, and earthquake hazards to improve prediction models and risk mitigation strategies. These efforts include developing more resilient building designs, strengthening emergency response infrastructure, and educating communities about earthquake preparedness.

Overall, the long historical record of earthquakes in Iran demonstrates both the destructive power of seismic events and the resilience of communities that rebuild after disasters. From ancient earthquakes recorded in early chronicles to modern instrumentally measured earthquakes, the list of earthquakes in Iran provides valuable insights into the geological processes shaping the region and the ongoing need for disaster preparedness and resilient infrastructure in earthquake-prone areas.
